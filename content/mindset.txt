Mindset: The New Psychology of Success
by Carol S. Dweck (2006)
Genre: Self-Help

════════════════════════════════════════

OVERVIEW

Mindset: The New Psychology of Success is a psychology and self-help book written by Carol S. Dweck, a professor of psychology at Stanford University. First published in 2006 by Ballantine Books, the book presents Dweck's research on the concept of "mindset" — the underlying beliefs people hold about learning and intelligence — and argues that these beliefs have a profound impact on virtually every aspect of a person's life.

The book's central framework distinguishes between two types of mindset: the "fixed mindset," in which people believe their basic qualities like intelligence and talent are static traits, and the "growth mindset," in which people believe their abilities can be developed through dedication, hard work, and learning. Dweck argues that the growth mindset leads to greater achievement and resilience.

════════════════════════════════════════

ORIGINS OF THE RESEARCH

Dweck's research on mindset began in the late 1980s, with a seminal 1988 paper that laid the groundwork for the framework she would develop over the next two decades. Her studies focused on how children respond to failure and challenge, revealing that some children view setbacks as opportunities to learn while others see them as evidence of permanent inadequacy.

Through extensive research with students of all ages, Dweck identified patterns in how people's implicit beliefs about intelligence shaped their motivation, effort, and ultimately their achievement. This research culminated in the mindset framework presented in the book.

════════════════════════════════════════

THE FIXED MINDSET

In a fixed mindset, people believe their intelligence, talents, and abilities are innate, fixed traits that cannot be significantly changed. People with a fixed mindset tend to believe that talent alone creates success, without the need for effort.

Dweck's research shows that people with a fixed mindset often avoid challenges because failure might expose their perceived limitations. They may give up easily when faced with obstacles, ignore useful negative feedback, and feel threatened by the success of others. The fixed mindset creates an urgency to prove oneself over and over, leading to a fear of being judged and a reluctance to take risks.

--- The Danger of Praise ---

One of Dweck's most influential findings concerns the effects of praise on children. Her research demonstrated that praising children for their intelligence ("You're so smart!") rather than their effort ("You worked really hard!") actually undermines their motivation and performance. Children praised for intelligence develop a fixed mindset, becoming less willing to take on challenging tasks for fear of appearing less smart.

════════════════════════════════════════

THE GROWTH MINDSET

In a growth mindset, people believe that their most basic abilities can be developed through dedication and hard work — that brains and talent are just the starting point. This view creates a love of learning and a resilience that is essential for great accomplishment.

People with a growth mindset tend to embrace challenges, persist in the face of setbacks, see effort as the path to mastery, learn from criticism, and find lessons and inspiration in the success of others. Dweck argues that virtually all great people have had these qualities.

The growth mindset does not claim that anyone can become anything simply by trying. Rather, it holds that a person's true potential is unknown and unknowable, and that it is impossible to foresee what can be accomplished with years of passion, effort, and training.

════════════════════════════════════════

APPLICATIONS

--- Education ---

The book devotes significant attention to how mindset affects learning and education. Dweck shows how teachers and parents can foster growth mindsets in children by praising effort rather than ability, teaching that the brain is like a muscle that grows with use, and framing mistakes as learning opportunities rather than failures.

--- Business and Leadership ---

Dweck applies the mindset framework to the business world, examining how fixed and growth mindsets affect corporate culture, management practices, and leadership. She profiles leaders who exemplify each mindset and shows how growth-minded organizations tend to be more innovative and adaptive.

--- Relationships ---

The book also explores how mindset affects personal relationships, including romantic partnerships and friendships. Dweck argues that people with a fixed mindset tend to believe that good relationships should be effortless, while those with a growth mindset understand that all relationships require work and growth.

--- Sports ---

Dweck examines the role of mindset in athletic achievement, profiling athletes and coaches who demonstrate how belief in the ability to improve through effort can lead to extraordinary performance.

════════════════════════════════════════

CRITICISM AND CONTROVERSY

Despite the book's enormous popularity, Dweck's mindset research has faced significant scientific criticism. Psychologist Timothy Bates of the University of Edinburgh conducted multiple studies attempting to replicate Dweck's findings and was unable to confirm several key claims, including the link between mindset and academic achievement.

In 2017, Nick Brown and colleagues applied the GRIM (Granularity-Related Inconsistency of Means) test to some of Dweck's published data and identified statistical inconsistencies in several studies.

A large-scale randomized controlled trial conducted by the Education Endowment Foundation (EEF) in 2019, involving thousands of students in English schools, found that a growth mindset intervention had no statistically significant effect on student achievement.

Dweck has responded to these criticisms by acknowledging that the concept has sometimes been oversimplified in popular application and that developing a growth mindset is more nuanced than simply telling people to try harder. She has emphasized that a "false growth mindset" — merely claiming to have a growth mindset without truly changing one's underlying beliefs — is a real and common problem.

════════════════════════════════════════

RECEPTION AND IMPACT

Despite the scientific debates, Mindset has been enormously influential in education, business, and popular psychology. The book has sold millions of copies worldwide and has been translated into numerous languages.

In 2017, Dweck was awarded the Yidan Prize for Education Research, worth approximately $4 million, recognizing her contributions to the understanding of learning and achievement. Her work has influenced education policy and classroom practices around the world, and the concept of growth mindset has become embedded in the vocabulary of educators, coaches, parents, and business leaders globally.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Carol Susan Dweck was born in 1946 in New York City. She earned her bachelor's degree from Barnard College and her PhD in psychology from Yale University. She held faculty positions at the University of Illinois, Harvard University, and Columbia University before joining Stanford University, where she is the Lewis and Virginia Eaton Professor of Psychology.

Dweck is one of the world's leading researchers in the field of motivation and has been elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and the National Academy of Sciences. Her research has been published in leading scientific journals and has been widely covered in popular media, including The New York Times, Time, Newsweek, and many other publications.
