by Yuval Noah Harari

ABOUT THIS BOOK

"Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow," published in 2015, is the ambitious sequel to Yuval Noah Harari's bestselling "Sapiens." While "Sapiens" looked backward at human history, "Homo Deus" looks forward, asking what might happen when the ancient problems that have dominated human existence — famine, plague, and war — are largely solved. What new projects will humanity undertake, and what new dangers will emerge?

Harari argues that having conquered (or at least significantly reduced) the traditional scourges of humanity, Homo sapiens will increasingly pursue three transformative goals: immortality, happiness, and divinity. Through biotechnology, artificial intelligence, and algorithmic decision-making, humans may fundamentally alter both their bodies and their societies in ways that could render traditional humanism obsolete. The book examines what happens when technology gives us godlike powers over life and death.

The book is a tour de force of intellectual synthesis, drawing on history, philosophy, biology, and computer science to construct a provocative vision of the future. Harari does not claim to predict the future with certainty, but rather to identify the trajectories and forces that are most likely to shape it. His goal is to prompt serious reflection about the choices that lie ahead, while there is still time to make them thoughtfully.

KEY THEMES

1. The End of Famine, Plague, and War — Harari argues that for the first time in history, more people die from eating too much than from eating too little, more die from old age than from infectious diseases, and more die from suicide than from warfare. This transformation opens space for new human ambitions.

2. Dataism — The book introduces "Dataism" as a potential new religion that venerates data flow and algorithmic processing. In this worldview, organisms are algorithms, and life is data processing. The universe's highest value becomes information flow.

3. The Useless Class — As artificial intelligence and automation advance, billions of people may become economically and militarily irrelevant. This "useless class" poses unprecedented social and political challenges.

4. The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness — AI demonstrates that intelligence can exist without consciousness. This has profound implications: if non-conscious algorithms can do most things better than conscious humans, what is the value of consciousness?

5. The End of Humanism — Liberal humanism, which places individual human experience at the center of meaning and authority, may be undermined by algorithms that know us better than we know ourselves. When an algorithm can predict your choices and optimize your outcomes, individual free will becomes questionable.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Yuval Noah Harari is a professor of history at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. Born in Israel in 1976, he specializes in world history, medieval history, and military history. His books "Sapiens," "Homo Deus," and "21 Lessons for the 21st Century" have sold over 40 million copies worldwide and have been translated into 65 languages. Harari is a regular commentator on global affairs and has addressed audiences at the World Economic Forum, TED, and the European Parliament. He is known for his ability to synthesize vast amounts of information into compelling narratives about the human condition.
