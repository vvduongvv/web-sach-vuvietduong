DIARY OF A WIMPY KID
by Jeff Kinney (2007)
Genre: Children's / Middle Grade Humor

ABOUT THIS BOOK

"Diary of a Wimpy Kid" is the wildly popular debut novel by Jeff Kinney that launched one of the best-selling children's book series of all time. Presented as the handwritten journal of Greg Heffley — a scrawny, scheming middle schooler — the book offers a hilarious and brutally honest look at the trials and tribulations of navigating sixth grade. Greg insists right from the start that this is NOT a diary; it's a journal. But whatever you call it, his entries are filled with the embarrassments, anxieties, and absurd situations that define the middle school experience.

Greg is not your typical hero. He's selfish, lazy, and constantly scheming to become popular — usually at the expense of his earnest, sweet, and clueless best friend Rowley Jefferson. Greg dreams of fame and popularity while doing as little actual work as possible, and his plans invariably backfire in spectacular fashion. From the "Cheese Touch" (a dreaded social curse involving a moldy piece of cheese on the basketball court) to his disastrous stint in the school play, Greg's misadventures are painfully relatable and laugh-out-loud funny.

What makes the book unique is its format: a combination of handwritten-style text and simple cartoon illustrations that perfectly capture Greg's voice and perspective. Jeff Kinney originally created the story as an online comic on Funbrain.com before it was published as a book, and its diary format makes it accessible and appealing even to reluctant readers.

KEY THEMES

1. The Middle School Experience — The book captures the universal anxieties of middle school: fitting in, navigating social hierarchies, dealing with bullies, and the desperate desire to be cool.

2. Friendship and Selfishness — Greg's relationship with Rowley is the heart of the book. Greg often takes Rowley for granted and uses him, raising questions about what it means to be a good friend.

3. Family Dynamics — Greg's interactions with his parents and brothers — the athletic older brother Rodrick and the annoying younger brother Manny — are hilariously realistic portrayals of family life.

4. Social Status and Popularity — Greg is obsessed with climbing the social ladder, and his schemes to become popular reveal how shallow and arbitrary middle school hierarchies can be.

5. Humor as Coping — The book's comedic tone shows how humor can be a way of processing the awkwardness, embarrassment, and confusion of growing up.

CHAPTER OVERVIEW

September — A New Year: Greg begins his journal as sixth grade starts, explaining the social hierarchy of middle school and his plan to rise to the top. He introduces his best friend Rowley, whom he considers socially hopeless but useful.

October — The Cheese Touch: Greg describes the "Cheese Touch" — a piece of moldy cheese on the blacktop that has become a terrifying social pariah marker. Anyone who touches it becomes untouchable. Greg also tries out for the school wrestling team, with disastrous results.

November — Halloween: Greg's Halloween plans go awry when his mother forces him to take his younger brother Manny trick-or-treating. A group of teenagers ambush them, and Greg and Rowley are humiliated. Greg blames Rowley for the fiasco.

December — The School Play: Greg manipulates his way into the school's production of "The Wizard of Oz," thinking it will make him famous. Instead, he is cast as a tree and is pelted with fruit by the audience on opening night.

January-February — The Popularity Project: Greg launches various schemes to become popular, including starting a comic strip for the school paper and trying to impress girls. Each attempt fails more spectacularly than the last. Rowley's own comic strip, "Zoo-Wee Mama," becomes the hit that Greg's never was.

March-April — The Safety Patrol Incident: Greg and Rowley join the safety patrol. Greg abuses his tiny amount of power, and when he terrorizes kindergartners with worms, Rowley takes the blame and is stripped of his badge. Their friendship fractures badly.

May-June — The Cheese Touch Resolution: As the school year ends, Greg and Rowley's friendship is tested to the breaking point. In a climactic scene, Greg sacrifices his social standing by claiming to have touched the cheese to save Rowley from bullies. The act of unexpected selflessness costs Greg his reputation but saves his most important friendship.

WHY READ THIS BOOK?

"Diary of a Wimpy Kid" is the rare children's book that appeals to virtually everyone — avid readers, reluctant readers, kids, and adults. Its unique format, mixing text with illustrations, creates an irresistibly readable experience that has turned millions of children into lifelong readers. Greg Heffley is not a role model, and that's precisely the point — he's a flawed, funny, completely recognizable kid whose mistakes and misadventures make readers laugh while subtly teaching lessons about friendship, integrity, and growing up.

Jeff Kinney created something special: a book that validates the chaotic, confusing, often ridiculous experience of being a kid. It says to every young reader: your life is worth writing about, even — especially — the embarrassing parts.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Jeff Kinney is an American author, cartoonist, and game designer. Born in 1971 in Maryland, he began developing "Diary of a Wimpy Kid" in 1998 as an online comic on Funbrain.com, where it received over 80 million views before being published as a book in 2007. The series has since grown to over 17 books, sold more than 275 million copies worldwide, and been translated into 65 languages. Four live-action films and an animated adaptation have brought Greg Heffley to the screen. Kinney was named one of Time magazine's 100 Most Influential People in the World. He lives in Massachusetts, where he owns a bookstore called An Unlikely Story.
