A People's History of the United States
by Howard Zinn

ABOUT THIS BOOK

"A People's History of the United States," first published in 1980, is a radical retelling of American history from the perspective of those who have been traditionally marginalized: Native Americans, African Americans, women, laborers, immigrants, and the poor. Howard Zinn challenged the conventional narrative of American history as a story of progress and freedom, instead revealing patterns of exploitation, resistance, and struggle that run through the nation's past.

The book begins with Columbus's arrival in the Americas and continues through to the early 21st century in later editions. Rather than focusing on presidents, generals, and industrialists, Zinn centers his narrative on ordinary people and social movements. He draws on primary sources — letters, speeches, diaries, and oral histories — to give voice to those whose stories are typically absent from mainstream history textbooks.

Since its publication, "A People's History" has sold over two million copies and has been widely adopted in university courses. While praised for bringing marginalized voices to the forefront of historical discussion, it has also been criticized for presenting a one-sided view that emphasizes conflict and oppression. Regardless of one's perspective, the book has indisputably expanded the scope of how Americans understand their own history.

KEY THEMES

1. History from Below — Zinn deliberately shifts the historical lens from elites to common people. He argues that traditional history serves the interests of the powerful by ignoring the experiences and contributions of the majority.

2. Class Conflict — A central theme is the persistent conflict between economic elites and working people throughout American history. Zinn traces how elites have used race, nationalism, and other divisions to prevent unified opposition to economic inequality.

3. Resistance and Social Movements — From slave revolts to labor strikes to anti-war protests, the book chronicles the long tradition of resistance in American history. Zinn celebrates ordinary people who fought for justice, often at great personal cost.

4. The Myth of Progress — Zinn challenges the idea that American history is a linear story of expanding freedom and democracy. He shows how gains for marginalized groups were won through struggle and often met with backlash.

5. Power and Propaganda — The book examines how governments and elites use propaganda, education, and media to manufacture consent and maintain social control.

WHY READ THIS BOOK?

"A People's History" is essential reading for anyone who wants a fuller picture of American history. Whether or not you agree with Zinn's political perspective, the book introduces crucial events, movements, and voices that are absent from most history textbooks. Understanding the Ludlow Massacre, the Haymarket affair, the resistance of Indigenous peoples, and the grassroots organizing behind every major social change gives readers a richer, more nuanced understanding of how America became what it is.

The book also models a different way of thinking about history — not as a series of dates and great men, but as an ongoing contest between those with power and those without it. This perspective is invaluable for understanding current events and for recognizing that the struggles of the present have deep historical roots.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Howard Zinn (1922–2010) was a historian, playwright, and social activist. He was a professor of political science at Boston University for 24 years. A World War II bombardier who later became a staunch anti-war activist, Zinn was deeply involved in the civil rights movement, working closely with the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) and participating in the Freedom Schools in Mississippi. His personal experience with both war and social activism informed his conviction that history should serve as a tool for social justice.
